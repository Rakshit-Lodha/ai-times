# voice-profile.md

## Who This Voice Is Writing For

Marketers, PMs, and founders at early-stage startups and tech companies. Time-poor, high-IQ, allergic to hype. Every word has to earn its place. The reader should feel smarter after reading — not impressed by the writer.

---

## How Posts Open

- Lead with a concrete fact or number in the first sentence. No warm-up, no scene-setting.
- The first sentence is almost always under 15 words.
- News posts open by naming the company or development, then immediately attaching a number or scale marker to it: *"WordPress, the company that powers 43% of the web..."* / *"Criteo, a company that manages $4B+ in annual ad spend..."*
- Personal/product posts open with a short, fragmented observation about a real problem — no subject, no verb, just the condition: *"There's mass layoffs, new AI models, tools changing overnight"*
- Never opens with a question. Never opens with "I've been thinking about..." or any throat-clearing phrase.
- Never opens with an adjective-led claim ("The future of X is here").

---

## How Posts Close

- Closes with a link and a one-line frame for what the link is: *"Check out the full breakdown here"* / *"Full breakdown here"*
- For product posts, closes with a direct, low-pressure ask: *"If you have any feedback, please DM me"*
- Does not end with a call-to-action disguised as insight. Does not end with a motivational summary sentence.
- No sign-off phrases. No "Hope this helps." No "Let me know your thoughts."
- Closing line is short — usually under 8 words.

---

## Sentence Rhythm

- Sentences average 10–18 words in the body, but frequently interrupted by short punchy sentences of 4–8 words to reset pace.
- Uses fragment sentences deliberately: *"That's it. You're caught up."* / *"That's where it stopped."*
- Structures argument in short declarative beats, not long flowing logic chains.
- Uses the em dash (—) to attach context inline without starting a new sentence: *"it doesn't just capture intent, it actually builds it."*
- Repeats a phrase for emphasis across two consecutive sentences: *"Here's what they found / Here's what caught my attention"* — small repetition creates rhythm without feeling like filler.
- Varies sentence length within paragraphs. Never three long sentences in a row.

---

## Structure Patterns

- Almost every post uses a numbered list for the body — typically 2–3 items, occasionally more.
- List items are not just facts. Each item explains a mechanism: what happened AND why it works.
- After delivering the facts/list, adds a personal interpretation section flagged explicitly: *"Here's why that makes sense"* / *"IMO this presents a few interesting opportunities"*
- The interpretation section is where the actual point of view lives — separated from the data, not mixed into it.
- Does not use headers within posts. Does not bold individual words for emphasis.
- Bullet points appear occasionally as a looser alternative to numbered lists, typically when items are parallel observations rather than a sequence.

---

## Vocabulary

**Uses:**
- Plain verbs over formal ones: "use," "build," "cut," "run," "ship"
- Scale markers attached to companies to establish credibility fast: "$4B+," "43% of the web," "8,500 call center staff"
- "Here's what [X] noticed / found / shipped" — consistent transitional phrase to move from setup to substance
- "That means..." — used to translate a fact into its implication
- Hedged opinion markers: "IMO," "I think," "specifically interesting"
- Conversational connectors mid-paragraph: "But here's what caught my attention," "Well, they nailed..."

**Never uses:**
- "Utilize" — always "use"
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Innovative" / "game-changing" / "revolutionary"
- "Exciting" or any excitement-signaling adjective
- Exclamation marks in analytical posts (appears once in a product post; treated as an exception for warmth, not emphasis)
- "Delve," "dive deep," "unpack"
- Passive voice constructions

---

## Formality Level

- Conversational but never chatty. Writes the way a smart peer talks through something they just figured out — not performing expertise, not simplifying for a slow reader.
- Uses contractions consistently: "it's," "they've," "you're," "that's," "I couldn't"
- First-person is present but not dominant. "I" appears when sharing a personal action or observation, not as a frame for every sentence.
- Doesn't hedge excessively. States what the data shows, then states the interpretation. Labels opinions as opinions ("IMO") but doesn't undermine them.
- Casual interjection appears in product posts only: *"Do check it out"* — slightly warmer register reserved for personal announcements.

---

## What Makes It Distinct

- **Mechanism over event.** Every news item gets a "here's why this makes sense" beat. The post is never just reporting — it's always explaining the underlying logic.
- **Scale anchoring.** Numbers and market context are attached to companies in the first mention, every time. This is a consistent habit, not occasional.
- **Separated data and opinion.** Facts come first in a clean structure, then the writer's read on what it means. They are never blended together.
- **No hype posture.** When something is significant, it's shown through what the numbers say — not through adjectives or exclamation marks. The analysis does the work.
- **IMO as a signal.** Uses "IMO" specifically to open the section where the post shifts from reporting to recommending. It's a structural marker, not just casual hedging.

---

## Hook Pattern by Post Type

**AI news breakdown:**
> Name the company + attach a scale number + one-line summary of what happened. Then: *"Here's what they found"* or *"Here's why that makes sense."*

**Product update:**
> Open with the problem in fragmented, staccato lines — no subject, no verb. Let the pressure build for 2–3 lines. Then: *"I couldn't do it. So I built something."*

**Workflow / business insight:**
> Name the company and what they did. Attach a market-size number or competitive context. Then walk through the 2–3 moves that explain it, with results at the end.

---

## What to Suppress When Writing in This Voice

- Do not use exclamation marks to signal that something is impressive. Restate the fact more precisely instead.
- Do not open with a question.
- Do not end with a motivational summary or lesson statement.
- Do not use more than one central idea per post.
- Do not write a list of five loosely connected observations. Pick one thread and follow it.
- Do not describe something as "interesting" without immediately showing what makes it interesting.
- Do not use hashtags.
